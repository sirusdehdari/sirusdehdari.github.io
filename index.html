Research & Background

Photo: Jaakko Meriläinen

I am an Associate Professor at the Department of Political Science, Stockholm University, and an affiliate of the Uppsala Center for Labor Studies at Uppsala University.

My research lies at the intersection of political economy and political behavior. I study why people vote for radical right parties, how identities form and change, and what drives — or suppresses — political participation across diverse populations.

My work has been published in the American Political Science Review, American Journal of Political Science, British Journal of Political Science, and American Economic Journal: Applied Economics, among others.

American Journal of Political Science 2024 2025 AJPS Best Article Award

Emigration and Radical Right Populism

Rafaela Dancygier, Sirus H. Dehdari, David D. Laitin, Moritz Marbach, Kåre Vernby

Emigration alters the composition of electorates, but also changes the preferences of those left behind. We document a positive correlation between emigration and support for populist radical right (PRR) parties across European precincts. Using Sweden as a detailed case study, we show that the departure of citizens raises PRR vote shares in places of emigration — and that the Social Democrats are the principal losers from emigration. We identify two mechanisms: emigration changes who votes and how the remaining population votes, with place-based effects driven by shifts in local socioeconomic conditions.

American Political Science Review 2022

The Ex-Factor: Examining the Gendered Effect of Divorce on Voter Turnout

Sirus H. Dehdari, Karl-Oskar Lindgren, Sven Oskarsson, Kåre Vernby

American Political Science Review, pp. 1293–1308

Drawing on Swedish population-wide data, we exploit the differential timing of divorces relative to election dates to identify the causal effect of marital dissolution on electoral participation. While both sexes are adversely affected by divorce, the effect on voter turnout is much more pronounced for men — nearly twice as large in the long term. This gender gap is largely driven by asymmetrical spousal mobilization: women have higher baseline turnout and thus provide stronger electoral encouragement within marriage, making their departure more consequential for their male partners' participation.

American Political Science Review 2021

Workplace Contact and Support for Anti-Immigration Parties

Sirus H. Dehdari

How does workplace exposure to immigrants affect support for anti-immigration parties? Combining detailed Swedish employer–employee registry data with precinct-level election outcomes for the Sweden Democrats, I find that a higher share of non-European coworkers has a negative effect on support for the Sweden Democrats. This effect is driven by same-skill contact in small workplaces — a setting that most closely resembles the cooperative conditions under which contact theory predicts attitude change — and is consistent with the hypothesis that repeated, equal-status contact reduces prejudice and outgroup hostility.

American Economic Journal: Applied Economics 2022

The Origins of Common Identity: Evidence from Alsace-Lorraine

Sirus H. Dehdari, Kai Gehring

We study the formation of national and regional identity using the history of Alsace-Lorraine — a region that switched sovereignty between France and Germany multiple times between 1870 and 1945. Exploiting the sharp border between the formerly German-administered territory and the rest of France as a natural experiment, we provide evidence on how state institutions, education, and shared civic experience shape identity formation over generations. Our findings highlight the malleability of national identity and the long-lasting effects of political incorporation.

British Journal of Political Science 2024

Granting Immigrants the Right to Vote in National Elections: Empirical Evidence from Swedish Administrative Data

Linuz Aggeborn, Henrik Andersson, Sirus H. Dehdari, Karl-Oskar Lindgren

British Journal of Political Science, pp. 712–729

We examine the effects of extending national voting rights to immigrants using rich Swedish administrative data. Sweden's path to citizenship and the variation in naturalization timing allow us to estimate the causal impact of enfranchisement on political participation and political outcomes. We find that granting voting rights substantially increases turnout among newly eligible immigrants and reshapes their partisan attachments over time, with implications for the electoral strategies of established parties.

Comparative Political Studies 2022

Economic Distress and Support for Radical Right Parties — Evidence from Sweden

Sirus H. Dehdari

Does economic hardship increase support for radical right parties? Using individual- and local-level data from Sweden, I exploit exogenous variation in plant closures to trace the electoral consequences of unemployment and income loss. I find that workers experiencing job loss are significantly more likely to support the Sweden Democrats — an effect concentrated among low-education male workers in deindustrializing regions. The results suggest an important economic channel in radical right voting that operates independently of cultural grievances.

International Migration Review Forthcoming

Age at Migration and the Political Integration of Immigrants — Evidence from a Sibling Analysis

Henrik Andersson, Sirus H. Dehdari, Karl-Oskar Lindgren

We study how age at arrival shapes the long-run political integration of immigrants using Swedish electoral data from 1994, 2010, and 2018. Exploiting within-family variation through a sibling design that controls for shared background characteristics, we show that immigrants arriving in early childhood (ages 0–5) are approximately five to ten percentage points more likely to vote in national elections as adults compared to siblings who arrived in early adolescence. These findings point to the formative importance of early civic socialization and schooling for immigrant political incorporation.
