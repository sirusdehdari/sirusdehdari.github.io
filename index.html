About

Research & Background

Photo: Jaakko Meriläinen

I am an Associate Professor at the Department of Political Science, Stockholm University, and an affiliate of the Uppsala Center for Labor Studies at Uppsala University. I hold a PhD in economics from the Institute for International Economic Studies (IIES) at Stockholm University.

My research sits at the intersection of political economy and political behavior. I study the causes and consequences of radical right voting — examining the roles of economic distress, labor market competition with immigrants, and the counterintuitive dampening effect of workplace contact on anti-immigration sentiment. A parallel strand of my work investigates political participation: how life events such as divorce affect voter turnout along gender lines, how emigration reshapes the preferences of those left behind, and how age at arrival shapes the long-run civic integration of immigrants.

I rely primarily on Swedish administrative data and quasi-experimental designs — including natural experiments, regression discontinuity, and panel methods — to establish causal estimates. My work has been published in the American Political Science Review, American Journal of Political Science, American Economic Journal: Applied Economics, British Journal of Political Science, and Comparative Political Studies, among others.

Publications

American Journal of Political Science 2024 2025 AJPS Best Article Award

Emigration and Radical Right Populism

Rafaela Dancygier, Sirus H. Dehdari, David D. Laitin, Moritz Marbach, Kåre Vernby

View Paper

An extensive literature links the rise of populist radical right (PRR) parties to immigration. We argue that another demographic trend is also significant: emigration. The departure of citizens due to internal and international emigration is a major phenomenon affecting elections via two complementary mechanisms. Emigration alters the composition of electorates, but also changes the preferences of those left behind. Empirically, we establish a positive correlation between PRR vote shares and net-migration loss at the subnational level across Europe. A more fine-grained panel analysis of precincts in Sweden demonstrates that the departure of citizens raises PRR vote shares in places of emigration and that the Social Democrats are the principal losers from emigration. Elite interviews and newspaper analyses explore how emigration produces material and psychological grievances on which populists capitalize and that established parties do not effectively address. Emigration and the frustrations it generates emerge as important sources of populist success.

American Political Science Review 2022

The Ex-Factor: Examining the Gendered Effect of Divorce on Voter Turnout

Sirus H. Dehdari, Karl-Oskar Lindgren, Sven Oskarsson, Kåre Vernby

American Political Science Review, pp. 1293–1308

View Paper

We study how marital dissolution affects political participation differently for men and women. Using Swedish population-wide data, we exploit the differential timing of divorces relative to general elections to estimate the causal effect of divorce on voter turnout. Although both sexes are adversely affected by divorce, the effect is much more pronounced for men — with the long-term effect nearly twice as large. The gendered disparity stems primarily from asymmetrical spousal mobilization: women's higher baseline voting propensity means they more frequently serve as the politically active partner in a marriage. When this dynamic is severed by divorce, men experience a greater decline in electoral participation.

American Political Science Review 2021

Workplace Contact and Support for Anti-Immigration Parties

Henrik Andersson, Sirus H. Dehdari

American Political Science Review, pp. 1159–1174

View Paper

How does increased immigrant presence in the workplace affect voting for anti-immigration parties? Combining detailed Swedish employer–employee registry data with precinct-level election outcomes for the Sweden Democrats, we find that the share of non-Europeans in the workplace has a negative effect on support for the party. This effect is solely driven by same-skill contact in small workplaces — the setting that most closely resembles the cooperative, equal-status conditions under which contact theory predicts attitude change. Our findings are consistent with the hypothesis that repeated intergroup contact reduces prejudice and hostility toward immigrants.

American Economic Journal: Applied Economics 2022

The Origins of Common Identity: Evidence from Alsace-Lorraine

Sirus H. Dehdari, Kai Gehring

American Economic Journal: Applied Economics, 14(1), pp. 261–292

View Paper

How do negative historical encounters with the state — including war, occupation, and authoritarian control — shape regional identity? We use the geographical division of the French regions Alsace and Lorraine to employ a regression discontinuity design at the municipal level. We find that communities with more negative experiences with nation-states develop a stronger regional identity in the short, medium, and long run. This is linked to preferences for greater regional self-governance, and the establishment of regional institutions emerges as a key mechanism in sustaining and reinforcing regional identity over generations.

British Journal of Political Science 2024

Granting Immigrants the Right to Vote in National Elections: Empirical Evidence from Swedish Administrative Data

Linuz Aggeborn, Henrik Andersson, Sirus H. Dehdari, Karl-Oskar Lindgren

British Journal of Political Science, 54(3), pp. 712–729

View Paper

Do immigrants vote less because they lack access to national elections, or for other reasons? Using Swedish administrative data and a regression discontinuity design, we compare recently naturalized citizens eligible to vote in national elections with those who naturalized just after the eligibility deadline. We find that turnout could increase by 10–20 percentage points if the voting rights of non-citizens were extended to the national level. Immigrants appear to abstain from local elections primarily because they perceive national elections as more consequential — suggesting that the level of election matters more than individual characteristics in explaining the immigrant turnout gap.

Comparative Political Studies 2022

Economic Distress and Support for Radical Right Parties — Evidence from Sweden

Sirus H. Dehdari

View Paper

This paper studies the effects of economic distress on support for radical right parties. Using Swedish election data, I show that a layoff notice among low-skilled native-born workers increases support for the Sweden Democrats by 0.17–0.45 votes on average. The relationship is stronger in areas with a high share of low-skilled immigrants — consistent with theories suggesting that economically distressed workers oppose immigration out of fear of labor market competition. Individual-level survey data corroborate these patterns: self-reported unemployment risk is positively associated with voting for the Sweden Democrats among low-skilled respondents, and negatively among high-skilled ones.

International Migration Review 2026

Age at Migration and the Political Integration of Immigrants — Evidence from a Sibling Analysis

Henrik Andersson, Sirus H. Dehdari, Karl-Oskar Lindgren

International Migration Review, 60(1), pp. 337–357

View Paper

We study how age at arrival shapes the long-run political integration of immigrants using Swedish electoral data from three elections (1994, 2010, and 2018). Exploiting within-family variation through a sibling design that controls for shared background, we find that immigrants who arrived in early childhood (ages 0–5) are approximately 5–10 percentage points more likely to vote in national elections as adults compared to siblings who arrived in early adolescence. The effect is partially persistent over the life cycle, pointing to the formative importance of early civic socialization and schooling for immigrant political incorporation.
